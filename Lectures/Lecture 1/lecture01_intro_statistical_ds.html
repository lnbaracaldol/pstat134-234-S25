Lecture 1: Introduction to Statistical Data Science

What is Data Science?

  • Data Science is a cross-disciplinary field focused on extracting insight from data through:
    • statistical modeling,
    • computational tools, and
    • domain-specific knowledge.

What is Data Science?

  • It integrates:
    • 🧠 The skills of a statistician to model and summarize data,
    • 💻 The skills of a computer scientist to store, process, and visualize data,
    • 🧭 The domain expertise to ask relevant questions and interpret results.
  • Goal: To combine these tools to extract actionable knowledge and support better decision-making.

What is Statistical Data Science?

  • A blend of statistics, mathematics, and computing for:
    • Understanding uncertainty
    • Modeling the data-generating process
    • Making interpretable predictions
  • Prioritizes interpretability and uncertainty quantification

Why Not Just Machine Learning?

  • ML often acts as a black box
  • Statistical Data Science gives you:
    • Probabilistic models with explainable components
    • Uncertainty estimates (e.g. credible/confidence intervals)
    • Frameworks for decision-making under uncertainty

Mathematical Foundations of Statistical Data Science

  • Linear Algebra
    Vector spaces, matrix operations, SVD, PCA — core for data structures and dimensionality reduction.

  • Calculus
    Derivatives, gradients, optimization — essential for training models (e.g., logistic regression, neural nets).

  • Probability Theory
    Distributions, expectations, joint/conditional probabilities — the language of uncertainty.

Mathematical Foundations of Statistical Data Science

  • Statistical Inference
    Confidence intervals, hypothesis testing, MLE, Bayesian methods.

  • Optimization
    Convexity, gradient descent, Lagrange multipliers — crucial for model fitting and regularization.

  • Numerical Methods
    Integration, sampling, MCMC — solving problems where closed-form solutions are impossible.

Statistical Data Science Workflow

A modern data science workflow centered around statistical thinking.

Statistical thinking isn’t a step — it’s embedded in every phase of the process.

1. Problem Definition

  • Clarify the goal: What question are we trying to answer?
  • What kind of decision or insight is needed?

Statistical thinking:

  • What is the population of interest?
  • What assumptions are we making?
  • What does a meaningful result look like?

2. Data Collection

  • Gather data via APIs, scraping, databases, surveys
  • Assess how the data was generated

Statistical thinking:

  • Is the sample representative?
  • What are the potential biases?
  • Is there randomness in the sampling process?

3. Data Wrangling

  • Clean, merge, reshape, and impute missing values
  • Prepare data for analysis

Statistical thinking:

  • Are we preserving the original structure of the data?
  • Are transformations introducing bias?
  • What are the implications of imputation?

4. Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)

  • Visualize distributions and relationships
  • Identify patterns and anomalies

Statistical thinking:

  • Are we seeing real signal or noise?
  • Do data patterns align with modeling assumptions?
  • Are there lurking variables?

5. Modeling

  • Fit models appropriate to the question (inference or prediction)
  • Evaluate assumptions and choose complexity wisely

Statistical thinking:

  • What are the assumptions behind the model?
  • Is the model interpretable?
  • Are we overfitting?

6. Model Evaluation

  • Use metrics, residuals, validation, and uncertainty quantification
  • Report confidence intervals or posterior distributions

Statistical thinking:

  • Are results generalizable?
  • How uncertain are our estimates?
  • What does the margin of error mean?

7. Communication

  • Present results using Quarto, R Markdown, slides
  • Include uncertainty and model caveats

Statistical thinking:

  • Are we communicating assumptions clearly?
  • Are we overstating what the data can tell us?

8. Decision Making

  • Use data to guide action or inform policy
  • Weigh costs and risks of different outcomes

Statistical thinking:

  • What are the consequences of being wrong?
  • What is the probability of making a Type I or II error?
  • How does uncertainty affect our decision?

R vs Python in Data Science

Feature / Use Case R Python
Origin / Strength Statistics, data analysis General-purpose programming
Syntax Style Functional, vectorized Object-oriented
Data Manipulation dplyr, tidyr, forcats, stringr , lubridate, data.table pandas, numpy , polars
Visualization ggplot2, plotly, lattice matplotlib, seaborn, plotly
Statistical Modeling stats, glmnet, brms, lme4 statsmodels, scikit-learn
Machine Learning caret, tidymodels, mlr3 scikit-learn, xgboost, lightgbm
Deep Learning keras, torch, via reticulate tensorflow, keras, torch
Reporting / Reproducibility R Markdown, Quarto, shiny Jupyter Notebooks, Quarto, dash
Community & Resources Academic, statisticians Developers, engineers, data scientists
Best For EDA, statistical modeling, Academic Research ML and Deep Learning, production and Deployment

R vs Python: What Are They Best For?

R: Best For

Task Why R Excels
Statistical modeling Designed for inference and diagnostics
Data visualization Elegant and expressive plotting
Exploratory analysis Fast and intuitive workflows
Academic research Optimized for publishing and teaching
Teaching statistics Formula interface and reproducibility
Reporting Integrated code + narrative

Python: Best For

Task Why Python Excels
Machine learning Scalable and modern ML ecosystem
Deep learning Widely adopted in AI research
Production systems Ideal for deployment and APIs
Scripting & automation General-purpose language
Interactive apps Tools for dashboards and web apps
Cross-domain integration Used in NLP, vision, web, robotics

These languages are complementary — not competing.
